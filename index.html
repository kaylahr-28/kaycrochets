The Beginnings

Progress is never linear!

It all started my sophomore year of high school. Fresh out of quarantine, I decided to join Fiber Arts Club- I loved creating things with my hands and I had never tried to crochet before. My friend also joined, but they already had some experience with crocheting. So, I asked them to help me learn. The crochet hook felt so unnatural in my hand. My first attempts to create something of substance consisted of awkward hand movements and plenty of wrist pain. Not to mention I was a lefty trying to learn from a righty's perspective, so I had to mirror all of my friend's movements in my head. Eventually, though, I had a basic idea of how to make a chain stitch, the foundation of many crochet projects... Then, I didn't crochet for a year and I forgot it all. The truth is I had been practicing different stitches with yarn provided by the club, but when I decided to undo all my work, the yarn got very tangled, so I spent the rest of the school year trying to untangle it. That's besides the point, though. I started crocheting again at the start of junior year, where I made my first actual project- a beanie! Since then, I've grown much more comfortable crocheting. I joined the Fiber Arts Club E-Board my senior year, and after high school I have continued to crochet throughout college. I still struggle with things like tension and gauge, but every project is a learning experience!

WHAT ABOUT KNITTING?

I also learned how to knit in my senior year of high school, and I can definitely see the appeal. It uses less yarn and 90% of projects are based on two types of stitches, making it easy to comprehend. However, I've not yet reached the point of comfortability where I can knit things without a pattern. I find it hard to customize knitting because it seems to follow such a specific pattern, in terms of where you start and end stitches. With crocheting though, I have much more freedom. I can stick my hook into any part of the project and just start building chains off of it, if I wanted to. I think there's so much more potential for creativity in crochet projects.

What is the Best Yarn to Use?

Well, that depends. The most common yarn to use is weight 4 acrylic. This is the standard for most basic crochet projects. Cotton is also popular, typically for lightweight clothing. These are the two that I normally use. I also like using chenille yarn, especially for amigurumi projects, because the material is softer and it also gives the figures a softer look. If you want to be fancy, you could try blended yarns made of two or more materials. I used a wool-acrylic yarn blend for a scarf once and it is very cozy! Not only should you consider material, you should also take weight, or thickness, into account. Weight 4 is the standard, but you could try bulkier yarns (weight 5 and above) or thinner ones (3 and under) to spice up your project! Bulky yarn is best for bigger projects because it works up much faster. Thin yarn can be tedious and cause some hand cramps, but they make your work look much more intricate. My advice would be: experiment! Find what materials amd weights are best for you. There's also nothing wrong with sticking with the standard- it is the cheapest option, after all.
